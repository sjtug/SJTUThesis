\section{Motivation}

The search for heavy neutral Higgs bosons with sizeable couplings to
top quarks provides a powerful probe of extended scalar sectors
such as the two-Higgs-doublet model (2HDM). As discussed in
Section~\ref{subsec:2HDM}, measurements of the properties of the
observed $125~\mathrm{GeV}$ Higgs boson constrain the model parameter
space to lie close to the alignment limit, in which the light
CP-even Higgs boson behaves SM-like. In this regime, the couplings of
the heavier neutral Higgs bosons $H$ and $A$ to top quarks can remain
sizeable and are enhanced at low values of $\tan\beta$.

For heavy Higgs boson masses above approximately $500~\mathrm{GeV}$ and
moderate values of $\tan\beta$, the dominant decay mode is
$H/A \rightarrow t\bar{t}$. Direct searches for heavy neutral Higgs
bosons decaying into top-quark pairs have been performed by the ATLAS
and CMS collaborations at centre-of-mass energies of 7, 8 and
13~TeV~\cite{ATLAS:2017snw,CMS:2019pzc,ATLAS:2024vxm}. However, inclusive searches for $gg \to H/A \to t\bar{t}$ are
experimentally challenging due to destructive interference with the
SM $t\bar{t}$ background, which distorts the resonance shape in the
$t\bar{t}$ invariant-mass spectrum~\cite{Gaemers:1984sj,Hajer:2015gka}.

An alternative probe is the associated production of a heavy Higgs
boson with a top-quark pair, followed by the decay $H/A \to t\bar{t}$,
leading to a four-top final state. Compared to inclusive
$t\bar{t}$ resonance searches, this topology is significantly less
affected by interference effects with the Standard Model background
and provides a distinctive multi-top signature.

The four-top final state has recently become experimentally
accessible. Using final states with two same-sign leptons or at least
three leptons, the ATLAS Collaboration measured the four-top
production cross section to be
$22.5^{+4.7}_{-4.3}(\mathrm{stat.})^{+4.6}_{-3.4}(\mathrm{syst.})$~fb~\cite{ATLAS:2023ajo},
while the CMS Collaboration reported
$17.7^{+3.7}_{-3.5}(\mathrm{stat.})^{+2.3}_{-1.9}(\mathrm{syst.})$~fb~\cite{CMS:2023ftu}.
Both measurements are consistent with the Standard Model prediction of
$\sigma_{t\bar{t}t\bar{t}} = 13.4^{+1.0}_{-1.8}$~fb computed at
next-to-leading order with electroweak corrections and threshold
resummation~\cite{vanBeekveld:2022hty}. A summary of the existing
measurements is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fourtop-summary}.
With the present experimental uncertainties, these measurements still
leave room for additional contributions to four-top production from
physics beyond the Standard Model.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/fourtop_summary_nov23.pdf}

    \label{fig:fourtop-summary}
\end{figure}

Beside extended Higgs sectors, the four-top final state also provides
sensitivity to more general new physics scenarios featuring new
particles that couple preferentially to the top quark. In particular,
top-philic simplified models, introduced in
Section~\ref{subsec:S1/S8}, predict new mediators such as colour-octet
scalars ($S_8$) and colour-singlet scalars ($S_1$) that can enhance multi-top production rates,
making the four-top final state a particularly sensitive probe of
such interactions.

\section{Analysis Overview}
\label{sec:analysis_overview}

To study four-top production experimentally, it is first necessary
to understand the decay topology of the four-top system and the
resulting observable signatures, which determine the possible
analysis channels. Each top quark decays almost exclusively via $t \to W b$, leading to
final states containing four $W$ bosons and four $b$-quarks.
The observable signatures are therefore determined by the decay modes
of the $W$ bosons.
Depending on whether the $W$ bosons decay leptonically or hadronically,
several distinct final states can be defined, as illustrated in
Fig.~\ref{fig:tttt_br}.

Final states with two same-sign leptons or multiple leptons (2LSS/ML) provide
excellent background suppression and have been used in searches for
heavy Higgs bosons in four-top final states by the ATLAS~\cite{2023_SSML_4top} and CMS~\cite{CMS-TOP-18-003}
collaborations. These channels correspond to relatively
small branching fractions. Final states containing one isolated lepton
or two opposite-sign leptons (1L/2LOS) have substantially larger branching
fractions, although they are experimentally more challenging due to
larger irreducible backgrounds. Nevertheless, the higher signal yields
in these channels provide strong discovery potential, and their
combination with the same-sign dilepton and multilepton channels can
further enhance the overall sensitivity of the search.

The analysis presented in this thesis focuses on the 1L and 2LOS
channels and builds upon the analysis framework previously developed
for the ATLAS measurement of Standard Model four-top production in these channels~\cite{ATLAS:2021kqb}. 
This strategy is further extended and refined to target heavy
neutral Higgs bosons produced in association with a top-quark pair
and decaying into a second top-quark pair.

A characteristic feature of both the signal and the Standard Model
four-top process is the large multiplicity of reconstructed jets and
$b$-tagged jets in the final state. Events are therefore categorised
according to the jet and $b$-tag multiplicities, defining multiple
signal and control regions that allow the background contributions to
be constrained from data.
Particular attention is devoted to the modelling of the dominant
background arising from $t\bar{t}$ production in association with
additional jets. This background is modelled using a data-driven normalisation and
multidimensional kinematic corrections based on neural networks.

To further exploit the complex event topology of four-top final states,
a multivariate analysis strategy based on a graph neural network (GNN)
is employed to enhance the discrimination between signal and
background. The signal extraction is performed through a profile
likelihood fit to the discriminant distributions in the signal
regions together with observables in the control regions, with
systematic uncertainties incorporated as nuisance parameters.

While the analysis is optimised for heavy Higgs boson production,
the results are additionally interpreted in terms of $S_{8}$ pair
production with masses in the range $0.4$--$2~\mathrm{TeV}$,
providing a complementary interpretation of the obtained limits.

\section{Data and Simulated Samples}

\subsection{Data Sample}
\label{subsec:data_sample}

The analysis uses the full Run~2 $pp$ collision dataset recorded by the ATLAS
detector at a centre-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s}=13$~TeV.
After applying standard data-quality requirements, the dataset corresponds to an
integrated luminosity of $139~\mathrm{fb}^{-1}$.

Events are selected online using single-lepton triggers, including both single-electron
and single-muon triggers, to ensure high efficiency for signal events containing at least
one isolated charged lepton in the final state.
Due to changes in trigger menus during Run~2, different trigger configurations are used
for different data-taking periods.
For each year, a logical OR of several single-lepton triggers is applied.
The full list of triggers used in the analysis is summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:slep_trigger}.

The effect of multiple $pp$ interactions per bunch crossing (pile-up) is
modelled in simulation, and the simulated pile-up distribution is reweighted to match
that observed in data.

\subsection{Monte Carlo Simulated Samples}

Monte Carlo (MC) simulation is used to estimate the signal acceptance
and to model the contributions from Standard Model background processes.
All simulated samples correspond to the ATLAS Run~2 simulation campaign
(MC16a, MC16d and MC16e), reproducing the detector and pileup conditions
of the data-taking periods.

Both signal and background samples include the simulation of pileup
interactions and are processed through either the full \textsc{Geant4}
detector simulation or the ATLAS fast simulation framework (AFII).
All simulated events are reconstructed with the same software chain as
used for the data.

\subsubsection{Signal Samples}
\label{subsec:signal_samples}

\begin{description}

\item[Heavy Higgs boson production (Type-II 2HDM):]

The primary signal process considered in this analysis is the associated production
of a heavy neutral Higgs boson with a top-quark pair, followed by the decay of the Higgs boson
into a second top-quark pair,
$t\bar{t}H/A \rightarrow t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$.
Signal samples are generated for a discrete set of heavy Higgs boson mass hypotheses
in the range $m_{H/A}=400$--$1000~\mathrm{GeV}$, covering the kinematic region
accessible with the full Run~2 dataset.

The hard-scattering process is simulated at leading order using
\textsc{MadGraph5\_aMC@NLO}, interfaced with \textsc{Pythia8}
for parton showering, hadronisation and the simulation of the underlying event.
The A14 tune is used for the modelling of the underlying event.
The decays of heavy-flavour hadrons are simulated using
\textsc{EvtGen}~v1.2.0.
The matrix-element calculation is performed in the four-flavour scheme
using the NNPDF3.1 LO parton distribution function set.
Spin correlations in the decays of the top quarks and $W$ bosons
are fully retained in the event generation.

The modelling of the heavy Higgs boson follows benchmark scenarios based on
a Type-II two-Higgs-doublet model in the alignment limit, as introduced in Chapter~\ref{subsec:2HDM}.
In this framework, the couplings of the heavy neutral scalars to up-type quarks
are enhanced at low values of $\tan\beta$,
leading to sizeable production rates in association with a top-quark pair.
Signal samples are generated assuming $\tan\beta \sim 1$
and no mixing between the scalar and pseudoscalar states,
such that the $H$ and $A$ bosons are simultaneously mass and CP eigenstates.

Samples are produced for heavy Higgs boson masses between
$400$ and $1000~\mathrm{GeV}$.
The corresponding widths are chosen according to the benchmark scenario
used for the signal modelling.
The mass points and widths used in the simulation are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:MassWidth}.
The total width of the heavy Higgs boson increases with its mass,
leading to a progressively broader resonance structure at higher masses.

Possible interference effects between the BSM signal process and the
Standard Model four-tops-quark production arise due to the identical final state.
Dedicated studies indicate that, within the sensitivity of the present analysis,
the impact of such interference effects on the signal acceptance and on the
observables used in the analysis is limited~\cite{Orlando:2258130}.
They are therefore not explicitly included in the signal modelling,
and the signal is treated as an incoherent contribution on top of the
Standard Model background.

The signal samples are normalised to a cross section of 12~fb, corresponding to 
the excess between the observed four-top production cross section and the Standard Model prediction.
Theoretical uncertainties associated with the choice of renormalisation and
factorisation scales and with the parton distribution functions are evaluated
separately and are included as systematic uncertainties in the statistical analysis.

\item[$S_{8}$ pair production:]
\label{subsubsec:sgluon_signal}

The analysis results are also interpreted in the context of the
$S_{8}$ model described in
Section~\ref{sec:sgluon_tttt},
where four-top production arises from the pair production of
colour-octet scalars followed by the decay
\(S_8 \rightarrow t\bar{t}\).

The $S_{8}$ signal samples are generated at leading order using
\textsc{MadGraph5\_aMC@NLO}.
The decay $S_8 \rightarrow t\bar{t}$ is handled by \textsc{MadSpin},
while parton showering and hadronisation are simulated with
\textsc{Pythia8}.
The coupling of the $S_{8}$ to top quarks, $y_{S_8}$, is set to $0.1$.
With this choice, pair production of $S_{8}S_{8}$ via strong interactions
dominates over the associated production process $t\bar{t}S_{8}$.
For $y_{S_8}<0.1$ and $m_{S_8}<2~\mathrm{TeV}$, the contributions from
interference with the Standard Model background and from $t\bar{t}S_{8}$
production are estimated to be below $10\%$ of the total cross-section
for $t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$ production via $S_{8}$, and are therefore neglected
in the simulation.
The kinematics of the $S_{8}S_{8} \rightarrow t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$ process do
not depend on the value of $y_{S_8}$.
Simulated samples are generated for $S_{8}$ mass hypotheses between
$0.4$ and $2~\mathrm{TeV}$, with a spacing of $0.1~\mathrm{TeV}$ in the
range $0.4$--$1.5~\mathrm{TeV}$ and $0.25~\mathrm{TeV}$ in the range
$1.75$--$2.0~\mathrm{TeV}$.

No dedicated optimisation of the event selection or of the multivariate
discriminant is performed for the $S_{8}$ interpretation.
Instead, the sensitivity to the $S_{8}$ model is evaluated by reusing the
analysis strategy and statistical model developed for the heavy Higgs
search.
\end{description}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This chapter presents a search for heavy resonances decaying to top-quark pairs in four-tops final states using the full Run~2 dataset recorded by the ATLAS experiment at $\sqrt{s}=13$~TeV, corresponding to an integrated luminosity of 139~fb$^{-1}$. 
Events with one lepton or two opposite-sign leptons and high jet multiplicities are analysed, with the dominant $t\bar{t}+\text{jets}$ background controlled through dedicated data-driven normalisation and multidimensional kinematic corrections based on neural networks. 
A mass-parameterised Graph Neural Network is employed to optimise the signal-to-background discrimination.

Upper limits at 95\% CL are set on $\sigma(pp \to t\bar{t}H/A)\times \mathcal{B}(H/A \to t\bar{t})$ for resonance masses between 400 and 1000~GeV, and are combined with previous multilepton searches. 
The combined limits range from 14~fb at 400~GeV to 5.0~fb at 1000~GeV, improving upon the multilepton-only analysis by up to 19\%. 
The results are further interpreted in the context of the $S_{8}$ pair production scenarios, excluding $S_{8}$ masses up to 1.3~TeV.

While these results establish competitive constraints on top-philic new physics scenarios, the sensitivity in the high-mass region remains primarily limited by the available dataset. 
The HL-LHC, expected to deliver up to 3~ab$^{-1}$ per experiment at $\sqrt{s}=14$~TeV, will substantially extend the reach of heavy-resonance searches in four-tops final states. 
Projection studies indicate that cross-section limits below 1~fb can be achieved for most resonances with masses beyond 1~TeV, with particularly strong sensitivity to coloured scalars. 
For benchmark $H/A$ signals near the projected sensitivity, discovery-level significances become achievable with the full HL-LHC dataset.

Together, the Run~2 search and HL-LHC projections demonstrate the strong and evolving potential of the four-tops topology as a probe of heavy resonances coupling to top quarks, spanning from the current TeV scale frontier to the extended discovery reach of the HL-LHC era.
