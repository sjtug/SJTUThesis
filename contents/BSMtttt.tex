\section{Experimental status of four-top production}
\label{sec:fourtop_experiment_status}

Four-top production is a rare SM process at the LHC.
Next-to-leading-order calculations predict a SM cross section of approximately
$\sigma(pp \to t\bar{t}t\bar{t}) = 13.4^{+1.0}_{-1.8}~\mathrm{fb}$ at $\sqrt{s}=13~\mathrm{TeV}$,
highlighting the experimental challenges associated with observing this process.

Each top quark decays almost exclusively via $t \to W b$, leading to final states containing four $W$ bosons and four $b$-quarks.
The observable signatures are determined by the decay modes of the $W$ bosons.
Depending on whether the $W$ bosons decay leptonically or hadronically, several distinct final states can be defined as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:tttt_br}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/4tops_BR.png}
  \caption{Branching fractions of four-top final states classified by the number of
  leptonic $W$ boson decays.}
  \label{fig:tttt_br}
\end{figure}

Final states with same-sign dileptons or multiple leptons feature significantly reduced backgrounds from SM processes, at the expense of smaller branching fractions.
These channels therefore play a central role in experimental measurements of four-top production.

The decay of four top quarks results in final states with exceptionally high object multiplicity, including a large number of jets and multiple $b$-tagged jets.
Figure~\ref{fig:tttt_jets} illustrates a representative jet multiplicity distribution in four-top events, compared to dominant backgrounds such as $t\bar{t}W$ and $t\bar{t}Z$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/4tops_jet_multiplicity.pdf}
  \caption{Representative jet multiplicity distribution in four-top production,
  illustrating the complexity of the final state compared to dominant backgrounds.}
  \label{fig:tttt_jets}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Run~2 observations by ATLAS and CMS}
\label{subsec:fourtop_run2_observation}

The ATLAS Collaboration reported the first observation of four-top production using the full Run~2 dataset at $\sqrt{s}=13$~TeV with an integrated luminosity of $140~\mathrm{fb}^{-1}$, selecting events with same-sign dileptons or at least three leptons and employing multivariate techniques~\cite{ATLAS:2303.15061}.
The observed signal significance is $6.1\sigma$, with a measured cross section
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(pp \to t\bar{t}t\bar{t}) = 22.5^{+6.6}_{-5.5}~\text{fb}.
\end{equation}

The CMS Collaboration also observed four-top production using $138~\mathrm{fb}^{-1}$ of Run~2 data in final states with same-sign dileptons and multiple leptons~\cite{CMS:2305.13439}.
The observed significance is $5.6\sigma$, with a measured cross section
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(pp \to t\bar{t}t\bar{t}) = 17.7^{+3.7}_{-3.5}(\text{stat})^{+2.3}_{-1.9}(\text{syst})~\text{fb}.
\end{equation}

Both measurements are broadly consistent with the SM expectation within uncertainties.
A summary of current measurements is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fourtop-summary}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/fourtop_summary_nov23.pdf}
    \caption{Summary of four-top production measurements (taken from Ref.~\cite{LHCTopWGFourTopSummary}).}
    \label{fig:fourtop-summary}
\end{figure}

\section{Analysis Overview}
\label{sec:analysis-overview}

The production of four top quarks represents one of the rarest processes predicted by the Standard Model at the Large Hadron Collider.
Owing to its small production cross section and complex final-state topology,
four-top production poses significant experimental challenges, requiring advanced analysis techniques and large datasets.
Using the full Run~2 dataset at $\sqrt{s}=13$~TeV, both the ATLAS and CMS collaborations have reported the first observations of this process in final states with same-sign dileptons and multiple leptons.
While the measured cross sections are consistent with Standard Model predictions within uncertainties,
the relatively large experimental and theoretical uncertainties leave room for potential contributions from physics beyond the Standard Model.

Motivated by the sensitivity of the four-top final state to new heavy states coupling to the top quark,
this analysis targets the production of a heavy neutral Higgs boson in association with a top-quark pair,
with the Higgs boson subsequently decaying into a second top-quark pair.
The resulting $t\bar{t}H/A \rightarrow t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$ topology provides a well-defined and experimentally accessible signature
for probing extended Higgs sectors with enhanced top-quark Yukawa couplings.
The study focuses on heavy Higgs boson masses in the range
$m_{H/A} \in [400,1000]~\mathrm{GeV}$.

Given the large Standard Model backgrounds and the high multiplicity of final-state objects,
the analysis is performed in final states containing either one isolated charged lepton
or two opposite-sign charged leptons (denoted as the 1L and 2LOS channels, respectively),
which offer a favourable balance between signal acceptance and background rejection.
The dominant background arises from top-quark pair production in association with additional heavy-flavour jets,
which exhibits similar final-state characteristics to the signal and constitutes the primary experimental challenge.

To enhance the sensitivity to the signal and to constrain background contributions directly from data,
events are categorised according to jet and $b$-tag multiplicities,
defining multiple signal and control regions.
Dedicated control regions are used to normalise and validate the dominant background processes,
while signal regions are optimised to maximise discrimination between signal and background.

To further exploit the rich event topology of four-top final states,
a multivariate analysis strategy based on a graph neural network (GNN) is employed.
The GNN is designed to incorporate information from all reconstructed objects and their correlations,
and is trained in a mass-parameterised approach to maintain sensitivity across the full signal mass range.
The output of the GNN serves as the final discriminant in the signal regions.

The signal extraction is performed through a simultaneous profile likelihood fit
to the GNN output distributions in the signal regions and to kinematic observables in the control regions.
Systematic uncertainties affecting both the normalisation and the shape of the signal and background predictions
are incorporated in the fit as nuisance parameters.
In the absence of a significant excess over the Standard Model expectation,
upper limits on the signal production cross section are derived.

While the analysis is optimised for heavy Higgs boson production,
the same analysis framework is also applicable to other new-physics scenarios leading to four-top final states.
The results are therefore additionally interpreted in terms of colour-octet scalar (sgluon) pair production,
providing a complementary interpretation of the obtained limits.

\section{Data and simulated samples}

\subsection{Data sample}
\label{subsec:data_sample}

The analysis uses the full Run~2 $pp$ collision dataset recorded by the ATLAS
detector at a centre-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s}=13$~TeV.
After applying standard data-quality requirements, the dataset corresponds to an
integrated luminosity of $139~\mathrm{fb}^{-1}$.

Events are selected online using single-lepton triggers, including both single-electron
and single-muon triggers, to ensure high efficiency for signal events containing at least
one isolated charged lepton in the final state.
Due to changes in trigger menus during Run~2, different trigger configurations are used
for different data-taking periods.
For each year, a logical OR of several single-lepton triggers is applied.
The full list of triggers used in the analysis is summarised in

\subsection{Signal samples}
\label{subsec:signal_samples}

\subsubsection{Heavy Higgs boson production (Type-II 2HDM)}

The primary signal process considered in this analysis is the associated production
of a heavy neutral Higgs boson with a top-quark pair, followed by the decay of the Higgs boson
into a second top-quark pair,
$t\bar{t}H/A \rightarrow t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$.
Signal samples are generated for a discrete set of heavy Higgs boson mass hypotheses
in the range $m_{H/A} = 400$--$1000~\mathrm{GeV}$, covering the kinematic region
accessible with the full Run~2 dataset.

The hard-scattering process is simulated using a matrix-element generator
that includes the leading or next-to-leading order QCD contributions,
depending on the production mode.
Parton showering and hadronisation are modelled using a dedicated parton shower program,
with matrix-element--parton-shower matching applied to ensure a consistent description
of additional QCD radiation.
All generated events include the full decay chain of the top quarks,
with subsequent decays of the $W$ bosons treated inclusively.

The modelling of the heavy Higgs boson follows benchmark scenarios based on
a Type-II two-Higgs-doublet model, as introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:theory}.
In this framework, the couplings of the heavy neutral scalars to up-type quarks
are enhanced at low values of $\tan\beta$,
leading to sizeable production rates in association with a top-quark pair.
For each mass hypothesis, the corresponding production cross section and total width
of the heavy Higgs boson are calculated consistently within the benchmark scenario.
The resulting values are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:MassWidth}.
The total width of the heavy Higgs boson increases with its mass,
leading to a progressively broader resonance structure at high masses.

Interference effects between the signal process and the Standard Model four-top
production arise due to the identical final state.
Dedicated studies indicate that, within the sensitivity of the present analysis,
the impact of such interference effects on the overall signal acceptance and on the
discriminating observables is limited.
They are therefore not explicitly included in the signal modelling,
and the signal is treated as an incoherent contribution on top of the
Standard Model background.

The signal samples are normalised using the central theoretical predictions for the
production cross section.
Theoretical uncertainties associated with the choice of renormalisation and
factorisation scales and with the parton distribution functions are evaluated
separately and are treated as systematic uncertainties in the statistical analysis.

\subsubsection{Sgluon pair production}
\label{subsubsec:sgluon_signal}

In addition to the heavy Higgs boson interpretation,
the same analysis framework is used to probe scenarios featuring
the pair production of a colour-octet scalar, commonly referred to as a sgluon.
Such particles arise in a variety of extensions of the Standard Model,
including supersymmetric and composite models,
and are produced predominantly through strong interactions.

Owing to their colour-octet nature, sgluon pairs can be produced with sizeable
cross sections at the LHC via gluon--gluon fusion.
If the sgluon couples preferentially to top quarks,
its dominant decay mode is $S_8 \to t\bar{t}$,
resulting in a four-top-quark final state from sgluon pair production.
This topology closely resembles the heavy Higgs signal considered in this analysis,
characterised by a high multiplicity of jets and multiple $b$-tagged jets.

The sgluon signal samples are generated for a set of benchmark mass hypotheses,
and are processed through the same detector simulation and reconstruction chain
as the heavy Higgs signal.
No dedicated optimisation of the event selection or of the multivariate discriminant
is performed for the sgluon interpretation.
Instead, the signal sensitivity is evaluated by reusing the analysis strategy
and the statistical model developed for the heavy Higgs search.
