\chapter{Large Hadron Collider and ATLAS Detector}

In order to directly probe physics at the TeV scale, as motivated by many extensions of the Standard Model, 
particle collisions with sufficiently high centre-of-mass energy are required.

While fixed-target experiments are limited by the kinematics of a boosted centre-of-mass frame, colliding-beam experiments allow the full beam energy to be exploited for particle production. 
This makes particle colliders the most effective approach to exploring the highest accessible energy scales.

For this reason, the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) was designed as a proton-proton collider operating at unprecedented energies, 
providing a unique opportunity to study both the Standard Model and potential new physics phenomena.
And the ATLAS detector is one of the two general-purpose detectors at the LHC, designed to investigate a wide range of physics processes.

\section{The Large Hadron Collider}

Being the world's largest and most powerful particle accelerator, the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is located at CERN near Geneva, Switzerland. 
It was designed and constructed as the flagship facility of the CERN accelerator programme to explore particle interactions at the highest achievable energies. 
The LHC is installed in a circular tunnel of 27 km in circumference and 100 m underground, originally built for the Large Electron-Positron (LEP) collider, and became operational in 2008.

The LHC is a proton-proton collider designed to operate at a centre-of-mass energy of up to 14 TeV. Protons are accelerated and guided along the ring by a system of superconducting magnets, 
including dipole magnets providing a magnetic field of 8.3 T to bend the beams along the circular trajectory. 
In addition to proton-proton collisions, the LHC is also capable of colliding heavy ions (Pb) beam for 2.8 TeV per nucleon pair, enabling a broad physics programme.

The proton beams circulating in the Large Hadron Collider are produced and accelerated through a sequence of pre-accelerators, collectively referred to as the CERN accelerator complex. 
This multi-stage acceleration scheme is designed to gradually increase the beam energy while preserving beam quality and intensity, before injection into the LHC.

The acceleration chain begins with a linear accelerator, Linac2, which provides an initial energy of 50 MeV to protons extracted from a hydrogen source. 
The protons are then injected into the Proton Synchrotron Booster (PSB), where their energy is increased to 1.4 GeV. Subsequently, the beams are transferred to the Proton Synchrotron (PS), 
which accelerates the protons to an energy of 26 GeV, before injection into the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS).
In the SPS, the proton beams are accelerated to 450 GeV and prepared for injection into the LHC. At each stage of the accelerator complex, radio-frequency cavities are used to increase the beam energy, 
while magnetic systems guide and focus the beams along the desired trajectories. The staged structure of the accelerator complex enables efficient accumulation of high-intensity proton bunches suitable for high-luminosity operation in the LHC.
Once accelerated to the injection energy of 450 GeV, the proton beams are transferred from the SPS into the LHC, where they are captured and further accelerated to their final collision energy. 
The CERN accelerator complex therefore plays a crucial role in delivering high-quality beams that meet the stringent requirements of the LHC physics programme.

Once injected into the LHC, the proton beams circulate in two separate beam pipes in opposite directions and are further accelerated to their final energy. 
Their trajectories are confined and guided by an extensive system of superconducting magnets, including dipole magnets that maintain the circular motion of the beams and quadrupole magnets that provide transverse focusing. 
This magnetic system ensures stable beam operation and allows the protons to be steered with high precision along the LHC ring.

The two counter-rotating beams are brought into collision at four designated interaction points distributed around the ring. At these locations, the beams are strongly focused to maximise the collision rate, 
enabling high instantaneous luminosity. Large particle detectors are installed around each interaction point to record the products of the proton-proton or heavy-ion collisions.

The four main experiments at the LHC are ATLAS and CMS, which are general-purpose detectors designed to investigate a broad range of physics processes, 
including precision measurements of the Standard Model and searches for new phenomena; ALICE, which is optimised for the study of strongly interacting matter in heavy-ion collisions; 
and LHCb, which focuses on precision measurements in the heavy-flavour sector. The overall layout of the LHC ring and the locations of the interaction points are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:LHC_layout}.
