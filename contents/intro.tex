Particle physics seeks to understand the fundamental constituents of nature and the interactions that govern them, and has long brought together the most advanced theoretical ideas and experimental techniques. 
On the theoretical side, our understanding of the microscopic world has developed progressively, 
from quantum electrodynamics and quantum chromodynamics 
to the unification of electromagnetic and weak interactions in the electroweak theory, 
completed by the mechanism of electroweak symmetry breaking, 
and ultimately consolidated in the Standard Model.
On the experimental side, the tools used to probe this world have evolved from early detectors such as cloud chambers and Geiger counters to modern, highly integrated detector systems. 
At the same time, the quest to probe the high-energy frontier has driven the construction of particle accelerators, allowing new particles and interactions to be created in controlled laboratory conditions, rather than relying solely on passive observation.
This evolution finds its most powerful realisation in the Large Hadron Collider, a 27-kilometre machine delivering the highest-energy particle collisions ever achieved, together with experiments such as ATLAS and CMS that represent the most complex detectors ever constructed.

This long-standing endeavour reached a defining milestone in 2012 with the discovery of the Higgs boson, marking the moment when the theoretical framework of the Standard Model and decades of experimental exploration converged in a single achievement.
Yet, the remarkable success of the Standard Model is accompanied by its incompleteness: 
a theory that appears so precise and consistent still leaves essential questions unresolved, 
both in its theoretical foundations and in its experimental description of the Universe. 
In this sense, the Standard Model may be viewed as a near-perfect description of nature, 
whose very perfection reveals its imperfections.
This incompleteness continues to motivate the pursuit of a deeper understanding of nature, embodying the spirit of blue skies research, where the quest for knowledge is pursued for its own sake.
In this context, searches for physics beyond the Standard Model provide a concrete and compelling direction to pursue these questions.

This thesis is centred on a search for new physics in four-tops production at the Large Hadron Collider, and is complemented by the development of reconstruction and unfolding techniques for differential measurements, as well as contributions to detector studies in the ATLAS experiment. 
The theoretical framework and the phenomenology of four-tops production are introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:theory},
followed by a description of the Large Hadron Collider and the ATLAS detector in Chapter~\ref{chap:instrument}, 
the reconstruction of physics objects in Chapter~\ref{chap:reco}, 
and the Monte Carlo simulation in Chapter~\ref{chap:simulation}. 
The main analysis, a search for heavy resonances decaying into top-quark pairs in the four-tops final state, is presented in Chapter~\ref{chap:BSMtttt}. 
The results presented here has been published in Ref.~\cite{ATLAS_tttt_2025_EPJC}.
This is followed by the development of a reconstruction and unfolding framework for differential measurements in Chapter~\ref{chap:Difftttt}. 
In addition, contributions to the ATLAS muon spectrometer, with a focus on Resistive Plate Chambers, are described in Chapter~\ref{chap:RPC}.
