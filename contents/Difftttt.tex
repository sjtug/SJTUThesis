\chapter{Differential Four-Top Production in Partial Run 3}

Following the inclusive observation of four-top-quark production in Run~2, the increased centre-of-mass energy and integrated luminosity delivered in Run~3 provide the opportunity to move beyond inclusive measurements and explore the detailed kinematic structure of this rare process.
Four-top production is characterised by high jet multiplicities, large hadronic activity, and complex decay topologies. Differential measurements of observables such as the four-top invariant mass, global event activity variables, and leading-object kinematics probe the underlying production dynamics in greater detail. In particular, the high-energy tails of multi-top observables are especially sensitive to potential deviations from the Standard Model prediction.

From a theoretical perspective, studies within the Effective Field Theory (EFT) framework have shown that four-top production provides enhanced sensitivity to higher-dimensional operators, notably through distortions in differential distributions at large momentum transfer~\cite{FourTopEFTStudy}. 
In particular, the invariant mass of the four-top system, $m_{4t}$, probes the overall energy scale of the event and is therefore especially sensitive to such effects. 
In these scenarios, modifications of the interaction structure can lead to characteristic enhancements in the high-mass region of the $m_{4t}$ spectrum, while inclusive cross-section measurements alone may exhibit limited discriminating power. 
Differential observables therefore offer complementary and potentially more sensitive probes of new interactions involving the top quark.

With the increased integrated luminosity delivered in Run~3, selected differential observables become experimentally accessible. 
This motivates the development of a dedicated reconstruction and unfolding framework for differential four-top measurements, which is the focus of this chapter.

\section{Study setup}

This section summarises the analysis configuration used for the differential four-top study presented in this chapter. 
The study is based on simulated samples and a simplified event selection designed to capture the key experimental features of the four-top final state while maintaining sufficient statistical power for method validation. 
The same configuration is used consistently throughout the reconstruction and unfolding studies described in the following sections.

\subsection{Data and simulated samples}

Signal and background processes are modelled using MC event generators interfaced with parton shower and hadronisation models. 
The simulated samples correspond to the partial Run~3 data-taking period (2022--2024) and provide the basis for constructing the detector response, evaluating systematic uncertainties, and generating pseudo-data used in the unfolding validation studies.

The nominal four-top signal process is generated at next-to-leading order (NLO) in QCD using \textsc{MadGraph5\_aMC@NLO} interfaced with \textsc{Pythia8} for parton showering and hadronisation. 
Alternative generator configurations are used to evaluate modelling uncertainties associated with the signal simulation. 
Background processes include $t\bar{t}t$, $t\bar{t}W$, $t\bar{t}Z$, single-top production, and diboson and vector-boson–associated processes.

\subsection{Event selection and analysis channel}

In the inclusive four-top analysis, final states containing same-sign or multi-lepton (SS/ML) signatures provide the dominant sensitivity. 
Final states containing two same-sign leptons or multiple leptons efficiently suppress the large $t\bar{t}$+jets background, resulting in significantly improved signal purity.
Within this category, the dileptonic same-sign (2LSS) channel offers a favourable balance between statistical yield and reconstruction complexity. 
Compared with multi-lepton final states, it benefits from a larger branching fraction while involving only two neutrinos in the final state, thereby reducing the degree of kinematic under-constraint.

These features make the 2LSS channel particularly well suited for developing and validating a reconstruction-driven differential measurement strategy. 
At the same time, the reconstruction and unfolding framework presented in this work is designed to remain extendable to multi-lepton final states, where the inclusion of additional channels may further enhance the statistical precision in future measurements.

Events are required to satisfy the following reconstruction-level selection:

\begin{itemize}
\item exactly two leptons with the same electric charge, with the leading lepton satisfying $p_T > 28~\mathrm{GeV}$,
\item at least eight reconstructed jets,
\item at least two $b$-tagged jets,
\item scalar sum of transverse momenta of reconstructed jets and leptons $H_T > 500~\mathrm{GeV}$.
\end{itemize}

No additional selection is applied at parton level. 
The unfolding procedure therefore extrapolates from the selected reconstruction-level phase space to the full parton-level phase space of the studied observable.

\subsection{Systematic uncertainties considered in the study}

In addition to statistical fluctuations, a representative set of dominant systematic uncertainties is included in the unfolding validation studies in order to approximate the conditions of the nominal likelihood fit.

The considered sources correspond to the dominant systematic uncertainties identified in the inclusive four-top analysis. 
For the signal process, these include the overall normalisation uncertainty of the $t\bar{t}t\bar{t}$ cross section (20\%) and modelling uncertainties associated with the event generator and parton shower. 
In addition, instrumental uncertainties related to flavour-tagging efficiencies are applied to all simulated processes.
Generator and parton-shower modelling uncertainties are evaluated using alternative signal samples generated with different Monte Carlo configurations, as listed in Table~\ref{tab:diff_mc_samples}. 
These uncertainties are propagated through the pseudo-experiments used in the pull and stress tests described in Section~\ref{sec:unfolding_validation}, thereby providing a realistic estimate of the impact of systematic effects on the stability of the unfolding procedure.

\section{Reconstruction of the Four-Top System}

Four-top production in the 2LSS channel remains characterised by a highly complex final state, featuring a large number of jets, multiple $b$-quarks, and two neutrinos from leptonic top decays. 
The combinatorial ambiguity in assigning reconstructed objects to their parent top quarks grows rapidly with jet multiplicity, and the presence of two neutrinos introduces additional kinematic under-constraints. 
A stable differential measurement of observables such as the four-top invariant mass therefore requires a reconstruction strategy capable of performing global object assignment while consistently incorporating neutrino kinematics.
The following sections describe the final-state assignment and neutrino reconstruction strategies adopted in this work.

\subsection{Hypergraph-based Final-State Assignment}

The assignment of reconstructed objects to the underlying decay products of the four top quarks is fundamentally a combinatorial problem. 
Multiple jets and $b$-tagged objects must be grouped into four overlapping decay chains, and incorrect assignments can significantly degrade the resolution of reconstructed observables.

Recent advances in machine learning have led to powerful reconstruction techniques based on attention mechanisms and graph neural networks, such as SPANet~\cite{Fenton2022Permutationless,Shmakov2022SPANet} and Topograph~\cite{Ehrke2023Topological}. 
These methods model collider events as sets or graphs of final-state objects, exploiting relational information to infer the most probable decay structure. 
While such approaches significantly outperform traditional $\chi^2$-based reconstruction~\cite{ATLAS2017TopMassAllHadronic},
their relational modelling is typically formulated in terms of pairwise interactions between objects. 
Multi-body decay topologies must therefore be reconstructed indirectly through combinations of two-body correlations.

In this work, the hypergraph-based reconstruction framework HyPER (Hypergraph for Particle Event Reconstruction)~\cite{HyPER} is employed to directly encode multi-body decay topologies.
The key conceptual difference between conventional graph representations and the hypergraph formalism is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:hyper_representation}. 
In a standard graph, final-state objects are represented as nodes connected by pairwise edges. 
In contrast, a hypergraph allows a single hyperedge to connect multiple nodes simultaneously, naturally representing multi-body decay structures such as a top quark candidate formed from three objects.

The reconstruction proceeds in two stages, as schematically shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:hypergraph_architecture}. 
First, a message-passing phase is applied on the underlying graph representation, allowing information to propagate between pairwise-connected objects and enabling the network to learn both local and global event features. 
Second, higher-order candidate structures are constructed as hyperedges corresponding to possible parent particles. 
These hyperedges are classified by the network, and the highest-scoring, non-overlapping hyperedges are selected as reconstructed top-quark candidates.

\label{fig:hypergraph_architecture}

This two-step procedure allows higher-order decay structures to be inferred on top of a learned relational representation of the event. 
Such higher-order relational modelling is particularly well suited to four-top production, where multiple heavy resonances decay simultaneously in a high-multiplicity environment. 
The hypergraph formulation therefore provides a natural and flexible representation of complex decay topologies and serves as the basis for the reconstruction-driven differential measurement strategy developed in this work.

The hypergraph-based reconstruction strategy is adapted to the specific decay topology of four-top production in the 2LSS final state.
As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:4tops_topology}, two top quarks decay hadronically, $t \to b q q'$, 
while the remaining two decay leptonically, $t \to b \ell \nu$. The reconstruction formalism is therefore designed to reflect this mixed decay structure.

For hadronic decays, all final-state objects are directly observable. Each hadronic top candidate is constructed as a hyperedge connecting three jet nodes. 
Within each triplet, pairwise relational scores are evaluated to identify the jet pair most compatible with the intermediate $W$ boson decay, 
while the remaining jet completes the three-body top candidate. 
This representation explicitly encodes the multi-body decay topology while allowing the internal structure of the candidate to be determined by the learned relational features.

In contrast, leptonic top decays involve an undetected neutrino. Since the full three-body system cannot be constructed from visible objects alone, 
leptonic top candidates are initially represented by pairwise edges connecting a jet and a charged lepton. 
The neutrino kinematics are reconstructed in a subsequent step using dedicated algorithms, after which the full leptonic top four-momentum is obtained.

This mixed hyperedge--edge representation enables the simultaneous treatment of fully visible hadronic decays and partially observed leptonic decays within a unified graph--hypergraph framework.
It therefore provides a flexible and topology-aware reconstruction strategy tailored to the complex final state of four-top production.

\subsubsection{Performance of the Final-State Assignment}

The performance of the hypergraph-based reconstruction is evaluated using a node-level truth matching procedure.
Reconstructed objects (jets and leptons) are matched to their corresponding truth-level particles using a $\Delta R$ criterion.
A reconstructed node is considered truth-matched if a corresponding truth-level particle is found within the $\Delta R$ matching cone.
For each reconstructed top candidate, the matching multiplicity is defined as the number of constituent nodes that are successfully matched to truth-level particles.

The reconstruction efficiency is evaluated as a function of the matching multiplicity.
For a given number $N$ of truth-matched nodes, the efficiency is defined as the fraction of candidates for which the reconstruction algorithm selects the combination containing the same set of truth-matched objects:

\begin{equation}
\epsilon_N =
\frac{N_{\mathrm{correct\ assignment}\,|\,N\ \mathrm{truth\ matched}}}
     {N_{\mathrm{candidates}\,|\,N\ \mathrm{truth\ matched}}}.
\label{eq:efficiency_definition}
\end{equation}

The distributions of the matching multiplicity for hadronic and leptonic top candidates are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:matching_multiplicity}.
These results illustrate the fraction of candidates containing different numbers of truth-matched nodes and provide an overall characterisation of the reconstruction performance.

\subsection{Neutrino Reconstruction}

While hadronic top candidates are fully reconstructed from visible decay products, leptonic top decays involve undetected neutrinos whose kinematics must be inferred from missing transverse momentum and mass constraints. 
In the 2LSS final state, two neutrinos are present, leading to an under-constrained system when only the measured missing transverse momentum is used.
To reconstruct the full leptonic top four-momenta, the neutrino kinematics must be inferred from the measured missing transverse momentum and reconstructed event information. 
Two complementary approaches are considered in this work: the Neutrino Weighting method, which searches for solutions consistent with the $W$-boson and top-quark mass hypotheses, and a machine-learning-based generative approach ($\nu^2$-Flows), which models the conditional distribution of neutrino momenta given the observed event features.
Both methods aim to reconstruct the neutrino momenta in a way that is compatible with the measured event information, thereby allowing the full leptonic top four-momenta to be determined and enabling the construction of fully reconstructed four-top candidates.

\subsubsection{Neutrino Weighting Method}

In the dileptonic topology, each leptonic top decay chain can be written as
%
\[
t_{1,2} \rightarrow b_{1,2}\,\ell_{1,2}\,\nu_{1,2},
\]
%
and the neutrino momenta are constrained by the invariant mass hypotheses
%
\[
m_{W,i}^2 = (p_{\ell_i}+p_{\nu_i})^2,\qquad
m_{t,i}^2 = (p_{b_i}+p_{\ell_i}+p_{\nu_i})^2,\qquad i=1,2,
\]
%
together with the transverse momentum relation
%
\[
\vec{p}_T^{\,\nu_1}+\vec{p}_T^{\,\nu_2}=\vec{E}_T^{\mathrm{miss}}.
\]
%
Since the longitudinal neutrino momenta are not directly measured, the Neutrino Weighting (NW) method~\cite{ATLAS2017TopMassAllHadronic} resolves the remaining degrees of freedom by scanning over the neutrino pseudorapidities $\eta_{\nu_i}$. 
In this work, $\eta_{\nu_i}$ is scanned in the range $-5\leq\eta_{\nu_i}\leq5$ using a uniform grid of 300 points. 
For each assumed $\eta_{\nu_i}$, the kinematic constraints reduce to a quadratic system, yielding up to two physical solutions for the transverse neutrino momentum components. 
Candidate solutions for the two decay chains are then combined and evaluated at the event level.

To incorporate the finite widths of the intermediate resonances, the $W$-boson and top-quark masses are not fixed to single values but are sampled from Gaussian distributions centred on their nominal masses.
The Gaussian widths are taken from the Particle Data Group (PDG)~\cite{ParticleDataGroup:2024cfk} values for the physical decay widths of the $W$ boson and the top quark. 
An optional jet energy smearing step can also be applied to propagate jet resolution effects into the reconstruction.

Each candidate neutrino-pair solution is assigned a weight defined as a product of Gaussian terms,
%
\[
w=\prod_k \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta_k^2}{2\sigma_k^2}\right),
\]
%
where the index $k$ runs over the set of constraints included in the likelihood. 
In practice, the weight includes contributions from the agreement between the predicted and measured $x$ and $y$ components of $\vec{E}_T^{\mathrm{miss}}$, and an additional term comparing the azimuthal direction of the inferred neutrino system to the measured $\phi(\vec{E}_T^{\mathrm{miss}})$. 
Further terms evaluate the consistency of the reconstructed invariant masses of the two leptonic $W$ bosons and the two leptonic top quarks with the sampled mass hypotheses. 
The resolution parameters $\sigma_k$ are motivated by the expected detector resolutions for missing transverse momentum and invariant mass observables, and are chosen to provide a physically meaningful likelihood discrimination among candidate solutions.
The neutrino configuration with the maximum weight is selected as the reconstructed solution for the event, from which the full leptonic top four-momenta are determined.

\subsubsection{Generative neutrino reconstruction with \texorpdfstring{$\nu^2$}{nu2}-Flows}

As an alternative to the constraint-based Neutrino Weighting approach, a machine-learning-based generative method, $\nu^2$-Flows~\cite{Raine2023nu2flows}, has been proposed to reconstruct neutrino kinematics in under-constrained multi-neutrino final states.
The method employs conditional normalizing flows to model the distribution of possible neutrino momentum solutions conditioned on reconstructed event information, allowing neutrino hypotheses to be sampled on an event-by-event basis.

As illustrated schematically in Fig.~\ref{fig:nu2flows_arch}, the $\nu^2$-Flows model consists of two components: an event feature extraction network and a conditional normalizing flow.
The feature extraction network embeds the collections of reconstructed objects (jets, leptons, and $\vec{E}_T^{\mathrm{miss}}$) into a single event representation vector, which is then used as the conditioning input to the flow to learn the corresponding conditional density of neutrino solutions.
To accommodate variable jet and lepton multiplicities, the feature extraction stage is implemented with an attention-based architecture.
Object-specific embedding networks map each physics object to a latent token representation, which is subsequently processed by transformer encoder blocks.
A learnable class token is used together with cross-attention to obtain a single global event vector, ensuring permutation invariance with respect to the input object ordering.

In the dilepton case, the conditional flow outputs a six-dimensional target corresponding to the three-momentum components of the neutrino and anti-neutrino, and can provide either the most probable solution or multiple sampled solutions per event.
% In this work, $\nu^2$-Flows is used as a complementary benchmark for neutrino reconstruction performance studies, while the nominal reconstruction employs the Neutrino Weighting method.

\subsubsection{Performance}

The performance of the neutrino reconstruction is evaluated by comparing the reconstructed neutrino kinematics to the corresponding truth-level neutrino momenta.
For each reconstructed solution, residual distributions are constructed for the neutrino momentum components and angular variables, $\Delta x = x_{\mathrm{reco}} - x_{\mathrm{truth}}$, 
where $x$ denotes $p_x$, $p_y$, $p_z$, $\eta$, or $\phi$.
In addition, the angular separation between reconstructed and truth-level neutrinos is quantified using $\Delta R = \sqrt{(\Delta \eta)^2 + (\Delta \phi)^2}$.

The residual distributions for the reconstructed neutrino momentum components are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:nu_residuals_momentum}, 
while the corresponding angular residuals and the $\Delta R$ distributions are presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:nu_residuals_angular}.

For the momentum components, the Neutrino Weighting and $\nu^2$-Flows methods show broadly comparable performance, with similar residual shapes observed for $p_x$, $p_y$, and $p_z$.
For the angular variables, the Neutrino Weighting method exhibits narrower residual distributions in $\eta$ and $\phi$, and also produces smaller values of $\Delta R$.

\subsection{Reconstruction of the Four-Top System}
\label{sec:top_reco}

The full reconstruction of the four-top system requires the determination of four top-quark four-momenta, comprising two hadronic and two leptonic decay chains. 
Following the hypergraph-based assignment and the neutrino reconstruction strategies introduced above, the complete event reconstruction is performed by combining these two components within a coherent workflow.

Two reconstruction workflows are considered, depending on the choice of neutrino reconstruction method, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow_comparison}.

\paragraph{HyPER + Neutrino Weighting.}

In the first workflow adopted in this work, HyPER is first applied to determine the final-state assignment of visible objects. 
Hadronic top candidates are reconstructed as hyperedges connecting three jets, while leptonic candidates are identified through $b$-jet–lepton pairings. 
The Neutrino Weighting method is subsequently applied to each leptonic decay chain to infer the corresponding neutrino momenta, after which the full leptonic top four-momenta are constructed.

\paragraph{HyPER + \texorpdfstring{$\nu^2$}{nu2}-Flows.}

In the generative approach, neutrino kinematics are first inferred using the $\nu^2$-Flows model. 
The predicted neutrino four-momenta are then incorporated into the event representation used by HyPER, allowing both hadronic and leptonic top candidates to be reconstructed within a unified relational framework. 
In this workflow, the leptonic decay chains are reconstructed as complete multi-body structures once the neutrino hypotheses are provided.

In both workflows, the four reconstructed top-quark four-vectors are combined to form the total four-top system, from which the invariant mass $m_{4t}$ and other kinematic observables are derived.

\section{Profile Likelihood Unfolding Strategy}

Differential measurements require the correction of detector effects that distort the relationship between the kinematic properties of the underlying hard-scattering process and the reconstructed observables measured in the detector. 
Finite detector resolution, reconstruction efficiencies, and event selection criteria introduce bin migrations and acceptance effects that must be accounted for in order to infer the underlying particle- or parton-level distributions.

Several unfolding techniques have been developed for this purpose, including matrix-inversion approaches~\cite{RooUnfoldInvert} and iterative Bayesian unfolding~\cite{DAgostini2010}. 
In this study, the differential cross section is extracted using the profile likelihood unfolding (PLU) method, in which the unfolding problem is formulated directly within the statistical framework of the likelihood fit.
The following sections describe the definition of the differential observable, the construction of the detector response matrix, and the implementation of the profile likelihood unfolding procedure. 
Dedicated validation studies are then performed to verify the stability and potential bias of the unfolding configuration.

\subsection{Definition of Differential Observables}

The primary observable considered in this study is the invariant mass of the four-top system, $m_{4t}$, defined at parton level. 
The parton-level top quarks are taken from the matrix-element record of the event generator prior to parton showering and decay. 
The invariant mass of the four-top system is constructed from the sum of the four outgoing top-quark four-momenta,

\begin{equation}
m_{4t}^{\text{parton}} =
\sqrt{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{4} p_{t,i}^{\text{ME}}\right)^2}.
\end{equation}

This definition corresponds to the kinematics of the hard-scattering interaction and therefore provides a direct connection to theoretical predictions. 
The parton-level definition also avoids ambiguities associated with particle-level top reconstruction.

At reconstruction level, a proxy observable $m_{4t}^{\text{reco}}$ is constructed from the reconstructed top-quark candidates obtained through the workflow described in Section~\ref{sec:top_reco}. 
The reconstructed distribution serves as the measured observable entering the unfolding procedure.

In addition to the parton-level invariant mass, exploratory studies have been performed for several particle-level observables, including the scalar sum of transverse momenta $H_T$ and the leading-lepton transverse momentum. 
These studies illustrate the flexibility of the unfolding framework for different observables. 
However, as the emphasis of this thesis is on the development and validation of the reconstruction and unfolding methodology, detailed particle-level results are not presented here.

\subsection{Profile Likelihood Unfolding Formalism}

The differential cross section is extracted using the profile likelihood unfolding approach. 
The unfolding problem is formulated within the same statistical framework introduced in Eq.~\ref{eq:Likelihood}. 
While the inclusive measurement employs a single signal-strength parameter $\mu$ to scale the total signal yield, differential unfolding replaces it with a set of independent signal-strength modifiers, one for each truth bin.

The likelihood becomes

\begin{eqnarray}
    L\left(\vec{n}|\vec{\mu}, \vec{\theta}\right) 
    &=& 
    \prod_{r \in \text{region}}
    \prod_{i\in \text{bin}} 
    \text{Pois}\left(n_{i,r} \mid 
    \sum_{j=1}^{N_\text{truth}}
    \mu_j \, S_{i,r}^{(j)}(\vec{\theta})
    + B_{i,r}(\vec{\theta})\right)
    \times
    \prod_{k\in \text{NP}}
    G\left(\theta_k\right).
\end{eqnarray}\label{eq:PLU_Likelihood}

Here, $S_{i,r}^{(j)}$ denotes the reconstruction-level template associated with truth bin $j$, obtained by folding the parton-level distribution through the detector response matrix. 
Each parameter $\mu_j$ scales the contribution of truth bin $j$, while $B_{i,r}$ represents the background prediction. 
The nuisance parameters $\vec{\theta}$ encode systematic uncertainties affecting both signal and background expectations and are treated in the same way as in the inclusive fit.

In this formulation, unfolding reduces to a simultaneous maximum-likelihood determination of the bin-wise normalisation factors $\mu_j$, with systematic uncertainties profiled coherently across all bins and regions. 
The unfolded cross section in truth bin $j$ is therefore given by

\begin{equation}
\sigma_j = \mu_j \, \sigma_j^{\text{MC}},
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_j^{\text{MC}}$ denotes the Monte Carlo prediction for the cross section in truth bin $j$. 
The fit therefore determines bin-by-bin scale factors that correct the predicted parton-level spectrum to best describe the observed data.

This formulation naturally incorporates detector response effects, background normalisation, and correlated systematic uncertainties within a unified statistical framework through the profiling of nuisance parameters. 
Moreover, multiple regions or channels can be combined in a single fit, allowing the differential measurement to benefit from additional constraints and improved sensitivity. 
Using the same likelihood framework as the inclusive analysis also ensures a consistent statistical treatment between the inclusive and differential measurements.

In the absence of additional constraints, this formulation is equivalent to an unregularised matrix-inversion unfolding. 
Regularisation can be introduced by adding penalty terms to the likelihood, as discussed in Section~\ref{sec:regularisation}.

% In order to determine the bin-wise signal-strength parameters $\mu_j$, the parton-level observable must first be discretised and connected to the reconstruction-level measurement through the detector response. 
% This requires a well-defined binning strategy and the construction of a response matrix encoding efficiency and bin migration effects.

\subsection{Binning Strategy and Response Matrix Construction}

The binning of the parton-level observable is optimised according to two main considerations. 
First, each unfolded bin must contain sufficient statistical power to yield a meaningful uncertainty on the extracted cross section. 
Second, the detector response should remain well conditioned, requiring a sufficiently large diagonal fraction in the migration matrix.

In the present partial Run~3 dataset, about 20 signal events remain after the full event selection. 
Given this limited statistical sample, a finer binning would lead to unstable fits and large statistical uncertainties. 
To retain sensitivity to shape variations while preserving fit stability, a minimal binning with 3 truth bins is adopted.

The bin boundaries are chosen to approximately balance the expected event yield across bins, resulting in the binning
\[
[0.0,\; 1379.243,\; 1813.773,\; 5000.0]~\mathrm{GeV}.
\]
With this choice, the expected yield is approximately uniform across bins, corresponding to roughly 6 to 7 events per bin at reconstruction level. 
Although the resulting statistical uncertainties approach the nominal tolerance threshold, the diagonal elements of the migration matrix remain above 0.5, ensuring a sufficiently stable detector response.
This binning therefore represents a compromise between statistical precision and detector-level stability and is adopted as the nominal configuration for the differential measurement.

The connection between the chosen truth-level binning and the reconstructed observables is described by the detector response matrix. 
More formally, the relation between the truth-level yields $T_j$ and the expected reconstructed yields $S_i$ can be written as
\begin{equation}
S_i = \sum_{j} R_{ij}\,T_j ,
\end{equation}
where $R_{ij}$ denotes the response matrix relating truth bin $j$ to reconstruction bin $i$. 
In practice, the response information can be provided either as the full matrix $R_{ij}$ or in factorised form in terms of a migration matrix and a per-bin reconstruction efficiency. 
An additional acceptance correction may be applied to account for non-fiducial contributions in the reconstructed distribution.

The migration matrix $M_{ij}$ encodes bin-to-bin migration from truth bin $j$ to reconstruction bin $i$. 
It is convenient to define a column-normalised migration matrix,
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{M}_{ij} = \frac{M_{ij}}{\sum_{i} M_{ij}},
\qquad \sum_i \widetilde{M}_{ij}=1,
\end{equation}
which represents the conditional probability for an event generated in truth bin $j$ and entering the reconstructed distribution to be reconstructed in bin $i$.

The reconstruction-level selection efficiency is defined for each truth bin as
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_j \equiv P(\mathrm{selected}_{\mathrm{reco}} \mid j_{\mathrm{truth}}),
\end{equation}
i.e.\ the fraction of events in truth bin $j$ that pass the reconstruction-level selection and contribute to the fitted reconstructed distribution. 
With these definitions, the response matrix entering the likelihood model is constructed as
\begin{equation}
R_{ij} = \widetilde{M}_{ij}\,\varepsilon_j .
\end{equation}

The reconstruction-level selection efficiency and acceptance as functions of the parton-level four-top invariant mass are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:efficiency_and_acceptance}. 
The efficiency reflects the fraction of truth events entering the selected reconstructed sample. 
The bin-to-bin migration pattern is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:migration_matrix}, where the diagonal dominance indicates that the chosen binning preserves a reasonably stable correspondence between truth and reconstructed observables.

If a fiducial truth-level phase space is defined, events outside the fiducial region may still pass the reconstruction-level selection and populate the reconstructed distribution. 
This effect can be handled via a per-bin acceptance factor defined on the reconstruction axis,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_i \equiv P(\mathrm{fiducial}_{\mathrm{truth}} \mid i_{\mathrm{reco}}),
\end{equation}
which quantifies the fraction of reconstructed events in bin $i$ originating from the fiducial region. 
This factor accounts for the contamination of the reconstructed distribution by events originating outside the fiducial region. 
In the present work, the unfolding is performed to the full parton-level phase space and no truth-level fiducial restriction is imposed as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:efficiency_and_acceptance}; consequently, this correction is not applied ($\alpha_i \equiv 1$). 
The formalism is nevertheless presented in this general form to allow future extensions to fiducial measurements.

\subsection{Regularisation Strategy}
\label{sec:regularisation}

As discussed in the previous section, the detector response matrix introduces migrations between neighbouring bins of the truth observable. 
In the presence of limited statistics, these migrations can lead to strong bin-to-bin correlations in the unfolded parameters. 
Events generated in one truth bin may be reconstructed in adjacent bins, giving rise to anti-correlations in the fit where neighbouring bins compensate each other while producing similar reconstructed predictions. 
This behaviour amplifies statistical fluctuations and can significantly increase the uncertainties of the unfolded spectrum.

To stabilise the unfolding solution, a regularisation term is introduced as an additional constraint on the likelihood defined in Eq.~\ref{eq:PLU_Likelihood}. 
The extended likelihood becomes

\begin{equation}
L_{\mathrm{reg}}
=
L(\vec{n}|\vec{\mu},\vec{\theta})
\times
G(\vec f(\vec{\mu})\,|\,\vec 0,\Sigma_\tau),
\end{equation}

where the function $\vec f(\vec{\mu})$ represents finite-difference combinations of neighbouring bins and $\Sigma_\tau$ defines the covariance of the constraint. 
In terms of the negative log-likelihood, this corresponds to an additional quadratic penalty,

\begin{equation}
-2\ln L_{\mathrm{reg}}
=
-2\ln L
+
\sum_i \tau_i^2 f_i(\vec{\mu})^2 ,
\end{equation}

where $\tau_i$ controls the strength of the regularisation.

The regularisation functions $f_i(\vec{\mu})$ are constructed from finite-difference operators acting on the unfolded spectrum. 
Two commonly used choices correspond to discretised first- and second-derivative operators:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{First-derivative constraint.}  
First-derivative regularisation penalises large differences between adjacent bins,
\begin{equation}
f_i = \mu_{i+1}-\mu_i ,
\end{equation}
thereby discouraging large local slopes in the unfolded spectrum.

\item \textbf{Second-derivative constraint.}  
Second-derivative regularisation penalises curvature,
\begin{equation}
f_i = \mu_{i-1}-2\mu_i+\mu_{i+1},
\end{equation}
and therefore favours locally linear behaviour of the spectrum.  
\end{itemize}

A practical complication arises for the second-derivative operator at the boundaries of the distribution, since it requires three consecutive bins and cannot be defined directly for the edge bins. 
Several strategies can therefore be used to extend the curvature constraint to the full spectrum:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{One-sided second-derivative approximation.}  
Additional constraints can be introduced at the edges using one-sided finite-difference approximations of the second derivative ($f_0 = 2\mu_0 -5\mu_1 +4\mu_2 -\mu_3$ at the lower boundary and the symmetric expression at the upper boundary). 
For sufficiently fine binning, these provide additional boundary constraints beyond the standard interior curvature term. 
In the present three-bin configuration, however, the one-sided second-derivative construction does not introduce additional independent constraints and effectively reduces to a single curvature combination across the full spectrum. 
In this limit, the second-derivative regularisation is represented by the single term $f = \mu_0 - 2\mu_1 + \mu_2$, up to the bin-indexing convention used in the implementation.

\item \textbf{Mixed boundary constraints.}  
Second-derivative constraints are applied to interior bins while first-derivative constraints ($f_0=\mu_1-\mu_0$ and $f_{N-1}=\mu_{N-1}-\mu_{N-2}$) are used at the boundaries.

\end{itemize}

The strength of the regularisation constraint is controlled by the parameter $\tau$, which determines the relative weight of the smoothing term in the likelihood. 
In principle, separate regularisation strengths can be defined for individual bins. 
In this study, however, a single global parameter $\tau$ is used for all bins in order to simplify the optimisation procedure.

To determine a suitable value of $\tau$, a scan of the regularisation strength is performed. 
Two metrics are considered: the global correlation coefficient and the average statistical uncertainty of the unfolded bins.

The global correlation coefficient quantifies the bin-to-bin correlations introduced by the unfolding procedure. 
For each unfolded parameter $\mu_i$, it is defined as
\begin{equation}
\rho_i^{\mathrm{global}} =
\sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{C_{ii}(C^{-1})_{ii}}},
\end{equation}
where $C$ denotes the covariance matrix of the unfolded parameters obtained from the fit. 
Small values indicate that a bin is largely constrained by the data itself, while values approaching unity correspond to strong correlations with neighbouring bins. 
As an overall measure of the stability of the unfolding result, the mean global correlation coefficient averaged over all truth bins is used.

The evolution of the mean global correlation coefficient and the average statistical uncertainty as a function of $\tau$ is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:tau_scan}. 
A clear minimum of the global correlation is observed for $\tau$ values between approximately $0.3$ and $0.7$. 
Within this range, the unfolding solution exhibits reduced bin-to-bin correlations while maintaining stable statistical uncertainties. 
Among the studied configurations, regularisation schemes involving first-derivative terms provide the most effective reduction of the statistical uncertainty.

\subsection{Validation of the Unfolding Procedure}
\label{sec:unfolding_validation}

The profile likelihood unfolding procedure can introduce biases in the extracted differential spectrum if the detector response, binning choices, or statistical limitations are not properly controlled. 
In particular, the unfolding relies on simulated signal samples to construct the detector response matrix, and the stability of the method must therefore be verified against possible modelling mismatches and statistical fluctuations. 
In addition, analysis choices such as the binning of the observable and the degree of bin-to-bin migration can affect the robustness of the unfolding solution.
To verify that the unfolding configuration adopted in this analysis does not introduce significant biases, a set of validation tests is performed using simulated pseudo-data. 
The validation programme includes closure, pull, and stress tests.

\subsubsection*{\textbf{Closure Test}}

The closure test probes the internal consistency of the unfolding setup using statistically independent samples. 
The nominal signal Monte Carlo sample is split into two subsets according to the parity of the event number, with one subset used to derive the detector response and the other treated as pseudo-data.

The pseudo-data are then unfolded using the corrections derived from the independent sample. 
Only statistical uncertainties are considered in this test, and background contributions are not included in order to isolate the behaviour of the unfolding procedure itself. 
The closure test is considered successful if the unfolded spectrum reproduces the true distribution within the statistical uncertainties.

To study the impact of regularisation on the closure performance, the test is performed both without regularisation and with the mixed first- and second-derivative regularisation term with strength $\tau = 0.4$. The corresponding results are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:closure_test}. 
Without regularisation, deviations between the unfolded and true distributions reach approximately 20\%, reflecting the large statistical fluctuations induced by bin-to-bin correlations. 
When regularisation is applied, the agreement improves significantly, with differences remaining within about 5\% across the bins. 
This demonstrates that the regularised unfolding provides a stable and unbiased reconstruction of the underlying distribution.

A set of 5000 pseudo-experiments is generated by fluctuating the nominal reconstructed distributions according to Poisson statistics.
The pseudo-experiments include the expected background contributions and the dominant systematic uncertainties considered in this study, thereby approximating the conditions of the nominal fit. 
For each pseudo-experiment, the fluctuated distributions are treated as pseudo-data and the nominal unfolding fit is performed.

\subsubsection*{\textbf{Stress Test}}

The stress test probes the sensitivity of the unfolding procedure to distortions of the underlying truth-level spectrum that are not described by the nominal signal model. 
Since the detector response and templates are derived from simulated events, the unfolding procedure relies on the shape of the nominal signal prediction. 
If the true spectrum deviates from this prediction, the unfolded result may be biased towards the Monte Carlo expectation. 
The stress test therefore examines whether such shape distortions can be correctly recovered by the unfolding procedure.

To probe this potential bias, pseudo-data are constructed by introducing controlled distortions in the truth-level $m_{4t}$ spectrum. 
This is achieved by reweighting events according to a linear function of the truth-level $m_{4t}$ value. 
The resulting weight is applied to the corresponding reconstructed event, producing distorted pseudo-data distributions at reconstruction level. 
The pseudo-data include the expected background contributions and the dominant systematic uncertainties considered in this study, thereby approximating the conditions of the nominal fit.

The truth-level reweighting function is defined as
\begin{equation}
w(m_{4t}) = 1 + s \left(\frac{m_{4t}-m_0}{m_0}\right),
\end{equation}
where the pivot point is chosen as $m_0 = 2~\mathrm{TeV}$ and $s$ controls the slope of the distortion and the stress scenarios with $s=\pm0.25$, $\pm0.50$, and $\pm0.75$ are considered.
The reweighted pseudo-data are unfolded using the nominal response matrix and likelihood model. 
The unfolded distributions are then compared with the corresponding reweighted truth-level spectra to evaluate the ability of the unfolding procedure to recover the distorted spectrum. 
% This test therefore quantifies the sensitivity of the unfolding procedure to shape distortions not captured by the nominal simulation.

To study the impact of regularisation on the robustness of the method, the stress test is performed both without regularisation and with the mixed regularisation configuration using $\tau=0.4$. 
The results of the stress tests without regularisation are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_test_noReg}, while the corresponding results obtained with the regularised unfolding are presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_test_withReg}. 
In all tested stress scenarios, the unfolded distributions remain consistent with the distorted truth spectra within the statistical uncertainties. 
No significant bias towards the nominal signal prediction is observed, both with and without regularisation. 
This indicates that the unfolding procedure is able to recover moderate shape distortions in the $m_{4t}$ spectrum and does not artificially pull the result towards the nominal Monte Carlo distribution.

In analyses involving multiple observables, stress tests can also be performed by deriving reweighting factors from one observable and propagating them to others to test the stability of the unfolding procedure against correlated shape distortions.
